Savage wizard was a broad term for wizards that lived beyond "civilization", in societies that were considered "primitive" the unifying factor between the disparate cultures host so such mages being low technological progress and a lack of agriculture. For example, it would be quite unlikely to encounter such mages in the Dalelands, if not alarming, They were distinct from witch doctors, a variation of shamans found in barbaric and savage humanoid societies that wielded both arcane and divine magics, though could be misidentified as such. The term could also refer to mages who originated from such cultures and maintained a connection to them whilst living in another land or culture.

Culture

Savage wizards were finely in tune with the nature, possessing a deep understanding of both plant and animal life. or unsophisticated, the perceived "primitivism" of a wizard's tribal culture was not reflective of its moral, ethical, or alignment values. A "savage" tribe could just easily be a peaceful community of farmers and herdsmen as they could be barbarians.

The regions and cultures in the Realms where one could encounter those labeled as "savage wizards" included Evermeet, among the tribal green elves, Though Mazticans were labeled by some as "primitives", there were wizards of this sort among them, These wizards were also distinct from the anagakok, tribal wizards found among the Ulutiuns of the Great Glacier.

Where these wizards were found, they often held positions of power in their tribe, being either respected teachers, tribal counselors, or even leaders. They oversaw a tribe's purely magical rituals and protections, sometimes coming into conflict with shamans or witch doctors. In some cases, due to either superstition or the accusations of those seeking to usurp power, a wizard's tribesmen may come to believe that he is consisting with fiends or meddling with dark forces. Whenever this occurred the wizard was at risk of facing expulsion, accompanied by a threat of execution should they ever return.

Abilities

In terms of the schools of magic, savage wizards lacked the natural aptitude for mastery of abjuration or enchantment, but typically excelled in conjuration, invocation, and the conjuration subschool of summoning.

They were also typically quite physically fit and highly versatile due to part of their lives revolving around wilderness survival. The wide range of disciplines they were skilled in included the likes of fishing, fletching, forestry, the handling and training of animals, herbalism, hunting, tracking, and trapping.

Additionally, savage wizards had a handful of magical specialties that set them apart from some spellcasters. For one they were uniquely skilled in crafting protective talismans, which took the form of small pouches of herbs meant to be worn around the neck on a leather cord. These pouches exhibited an effect identical to the spell protection from evil and functioned for a full day, after which the herbs inside the pouch would disintegrate. Any manner of magic cancelling effects, such as dispel magic, could also end the effects of a talisman prematurely.

They were also skilled in constructing effigies, tall clay dolls crafted in the likeness of another individual and containing some small organic material from the target, such as a lock of their hair or a fingernail. Regardless of their distance to the effigy, anything afflicted upon it would be suffered by the victim to whose likeness they were modeled after. This effigy would eventually disintegrate after either inflicting a certain amount of harm, being affected by dispel magic or similar effects, or simply after a whole tenday had passed since its creation.

Lastly, savage wizards were skilled at divining a major undertaking's fate by interpreting various omens found in nature. For example, gathering clouds, a swarm of insects, and veins on a leaf could all potentially be interpreted as omens. These mages typically consulted the omens of nature before embarking on a journey, engaging in a major battle, or setting out on any form of significant task. If the omens were found to be vague, they were considered neutral, but they were otherwise ranged from auspicious and favorable to ill omens and warnings of great danger ahead of the spellcaster.

Combat

When not casting spells a savage wizard typically wielded either a blowgun (mainly in jungle environs), dagger, knife, or sling.
