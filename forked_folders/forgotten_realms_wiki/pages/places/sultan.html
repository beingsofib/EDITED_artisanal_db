Sultan

Sultan ( most often cultures in desert environments.

Zakharan Titles

In the vast lands of Zakhara, "sultan" was used by some to refer to a sovereign ruler, equivalent to a king. This tradition dated back long before the discovery of the Law of the Loregiver and the founding of the Enlightened Throne. From the lands around the Golden Gulf, the Pearl Cities, to some of the Free Cities of North Zakhara, like Qadib and Simbaya, to Hiyal, and to the isles of the Corsair Domains in times long past. Under the Enlightened Throne they were a step below the Grand Caliph, ruling its many city-states and small empires. But where they ranked socially and politically amongst the plethora of other lesser rulers in Zakhara, like the khedives or padishahs, was a matter of debate and personal opinion.

Calishite Titles

In Faerûn, the title was prominently used in the nation of Calimshan, From the Sixth Age under the Shoon Imperium and onward, the title was used to refer to what other lands would call "princes". Or more accurately "military princes", with vizars being "merchant princes". This was in contrast to Zakhara, where the title "emir" was typically used to refer to a prince. Most also held lesser titles, typically being honorary military ranks, such as qayadin (general) or ralbahr (admiral), which had far less social or political weight behind them.

Calimshan's sultans were a social rank below the nation's own caleph, the Syl-pasha, whose many official titles included "Syl-Sultan of the Cities". Some were direct kin of the syl-pasha, whilst others were merely good friends or allies. Their social status was equal to that of el ynamalikkar, the "lesser princes". However, the political status of sultans was often seen as far greater, being nearly as revered as the syl-pasha, for unlike the ynamalikkar the sultans were less disconnected from everyday society.

Sultans sort of acted as the "rulers" of Calishite cities, overseeing the sabbalads (mayors) and their sabbans to a limited degree. And in the capital city of Calimport, which held the moniker "City of Sultans", sultans oversaw its many wards. who spent time making sure that the scheming sultans of Almraiven and Memnon were kept in check.

A Calishite sultan's authority also extended to el Amlakkar and el Farisan branches of the Calishite armed forces. Amlak captains had to report to sultans and in some cases, when people of great status were arrested, they might be brought to the sultan's home to be kept prisoner. In some cases their will was considered lawful enough to decide what was criminal and what was a fitting punishment.

Beyond Calimshan, there was the Arnaden region of south Faerûn, whose cultures were heavily influenced by the Calishites. There many nobles could trace their lineage back to ancient Calishite sultans. The Arnadar city-state of Tulmon was ruled by a sultan. And many commoners, Calishites, and outsiders used "sultan" to refer to the ruler of Mintar, even though the official title of that city-state's rulers was "Grand Murlistar".

Other Sultanates

Other desert lands in the Realms that were host to sultanates included the lost kingdom of Rhentria in the Anauroch, the Bedine tribe Desai in the Anauroch, and the city of Pazar in ancient Bakar. Beyond deserts, the title was used by the Laska tribe of Shou Lung's Kilap Valley for their ruler.

Beyond the Toril, the title "sultan" was used for the ruler of all efreeti and their famous City of Brass. In terms of the other genie races, the Great Khan of the Dao was sometimes referred to as the "Stone Sultan" and some marids claimed the title of sultan. Though as marids had no set order to their various self-professed rankings, their society was plagued by a hodgepodge of conflicting hierarchies and precedents.

Trivia

  • At some point the criminal organization known as the Night Masks divided their members up into three cadres, each named after a particular monarchical title. These were the Deys of Night, the Sultans of Sunset, and the Midnight Maharajas.
