Jewel Ward was an upper-class district of Calimport and was one of the city's seven Central Wards. It stood out as the predominant home of Calimport's gemcutters and whitesmiths, though this inherent labor-focused aspect of the ward made it considered less prestigious by the "noble" denizens of the neighboring Grand and Palace Wards.

Location

This area was located in the western center of the city, sandwiched between Grand Ward to the east and Palace Ward to the west. To the north it was bordered by the Faiths and Trades Wards. And to the south it bordered the Dock Ward.

History

The area was formerly part of separate city quarters, before Syl-Pasha Ralan el Pesarkhal established the system of wards in the , superseding the long-standing system of sabbans and drudachs. Leading up to this point, over the course of a few centuries the areas that would be delineated as the Grand, Jewel, and Palace wards underwent vertical construction by the Circle of Architects, Engineers, and Builders. This was done with the intention of providing both the upper classes and their servants greater areas of mobility, as well as to keep the latter out of sight.

Government

In the late 14th century DR, the Sultan of Jewel Ward was Janar yn Janar el Vyrsaty. And as one of the high priests of the Temple of Old Night, he had a myriad of influence in the Muzhahajaarnadah, which he leveraged to pit certain elements against one another for his own personal gain.

Trade

Over 60% of Calimport's black-market trade in weapons and magic goods was conducted on the understreets of Jewel Ward. The lower levels of legitimate businesses were also known to deal in the same goods that they peddled on the upperstreets of Jewel Ward, just with far less reservation about who they sold to. Shops that did business on the lower level also had less reservations about what wares they sold, how they disguised their wares, or even how they altered them.

Inhabitants

The lower level of the ward, out of sight of the upper classes, was reserved for their servants and slaves. Life here largely operated as any other part of Calimport, but due to its obscured nature many sultans, pashas, and the like conducted clandestine activities there with the city's criminal powers.

Sabbans

Jewel Ward was divided into three sabbans and nine drudachs. However, much like its neighbors, the distinctions between these sabbans and drudachs was blurred due to its layered infrastructure.

The slightly vaulted, stonework secondary layer of Jewel Ward was the "ground level" to its rich denizens. Braced by both stone and timber supports, it was comprised of solid street avenues and courts, granting the upper classes more uninterrupted movement than the sabban-wall byways afforded. The engineers responsible constructed it in such a manner as to ensure that it would stay elevated even if half of these timber supports were broken. One could access the city's true ground level and vice-versa by traversing sets of stairs.

The edges of the ward's byways, served as the "curbs" of this layer. As the boundary defining sabban marks were not present on the second layer, those that walked its elevated streets used the glazed coloration of these curbs to determine which sabban and drudach they were standing in. Each were assigned a distinct coloration, with the latter being denoted as a stripe at the base of the sabban color.

The lower level of Jewel Ward was often lumped together with those of the Grand and Palace wards as the "Upper Muzad", being considered the uppermost layer of the Muzhahajaarnadah. In some areas there were even said to be hidden entrances to the Muzad. With the sky blocked out by the elevated streets, save for stairwells and the gates to the open-air Outer or Port Wards, torches had to be relied upon for illumination.

Kalil's Sabban

Kalil's Sabban was the foremost sector of inscribing and fine detailing, home to artists that created quite ornate hilts and weapons. It was also considered the place to go if one desired concealed weaponry, hidden with jewelry or other such items.

The sabban's name was considered somewhat atypical of sabban naming conventions. Both it and its many aliases—Butcher's Sabban, Sholehfall Sabban, and Vihad Sabban—were all derived from Kalil Vihad, a brutal warrior pasha during the Forth Age of Calimshan who destroyed the Sholeh Dynasty.

Reflecting the brutal origins of its name, the mark of Kalil's Sabban was a pair of scimitars, crossed beneath a skull. The color displayed on the edges of its walls was a deep purple, whilst the colors at their base representing its drudachs were black, gray, and white. The three drudachs that made up the sabban were Ehraam Drudach, Solem Drudach, and Yuzhad Drudach.

Mjôm Sabban

Also known as the "The Marriage Sabban", due in part to its predominant industries of silk importation and jewelry crafting and selling.

The other inspiration for the name was a particularly gruesome murder of a bridal procession that occurred in -187 DR. Whilst en-route to the House of the Holy Dawn, the procession was murdered by the groom's political rivals. Since that day, the victims' ghosts would manifest in the understreets of Mjôm Sabban upon the anniversary of their murder. Their bodies would begin as white and insubstantial in form, shifting into a red shade the closer they got to the side of their murder, until finally fading away with mournful wails as they got within of the temple.

The sabban mark of Mjôm consisted of two linked rings, set upon a diamond field. The color displayed on the edges of its walls was blaze orange, whilst the colors at their base representing its drudachs were russet red, slate gray, and sunflower yellow. The three drudachs that made up the sabban were Akklah Drudach, Ginayna Drudach, and Kadaladh Drudach.

Nadana Sabban

Also known as the "Gem Sabban", for this sector of the ward was the predominant home of gemcutters and the city's gem trade. Being that such a high value commodity was dealt with in this sabban, it had a far higher presence of amlakkar and mercenary guards than anywhere else in the city, save for the Pasha's Sabban. Over half of its residents were guards, with the interests in either certain guilds or families. And what few khanduqs were present were all magically protected, ensuring both raw and finished gems were safely stored.

The sabban mark of Nadana was a gem cut in crown fashion, with seven facets. The color displayed on the edges of its walls was white, whilst the colors at their base representing its drudachs were emerald green, royal blue, and royal purple. The three drudachs that made up the sabban were Octel Drudach, Parlial Drudach, and Tiagar Drudach.
