Gods of the four directions

Venerated among the wu-jen of T'u Lung, these four deities were each associated with one of the four cardinal directions and one of the five elemental building blocks of matter, but collectively held dominion over the wind. It was believed that proper veneration of them was essential to mastering elemental air.

Culture

In addition to the five elements, T'u Lung culture traditionally associated a particular animal, color, clade of creatures, grain, organ, number, season, smell, and taste to each of the cardinal directions. Memorizing these "correlates" was considered by some wu jen to be a necessary component in mastering magic.

Members

Chu Jung: The god of the South. They were traditionally depicted as some form of beast with the face of a human, riding upon the backs of two flaming snakes that had the faces of human women. Their direction was associated with fire.

Ju Shou: The god of the West. He was traditionally depicted as a human male that wore a snake on his left ear and rode upon the backs of two lung dragons. His direction was associated with metal.

Kou Mang: The god of the East. They were traditionally depicted with the body of a bird and the face of a human. Like Ju Shou, they were also depicted riding upon two lung dragons. Their direction was associated with wood.

Yu Chiang: The god of the North. He was traditionally depicted as a human with black wings, resembling those of a hawk, and feathers in place of hair. A small green snake was worn on each of his ears and a larger green snake was coiled around each of his legs. His direction was associated with water.
