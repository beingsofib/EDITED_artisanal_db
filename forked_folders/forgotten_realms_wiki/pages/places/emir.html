Emir

Emir ( The state and territories ruled by an emir, as well as their office, were sometimes referred to as an emirate or amirate.

Faerûnian Titles

In the desert of Anauroch, the title of "emir" was used by the Bedine to refer to a provisionary position used in times of crisis. An emir was the leader of a union of several tribes, acting as an army against outside threats, such as asabi, lamias, and the Zhentarim.

In west Faerûn's nation of Calimshan, use of the title "emir" began in the Second Age of Calimshan under the Tavihran Dynasty, used to refer to those who governed the territories north of Coramshan on behalf of the syl-pasha of Calimport and later the qysar. This included the Purple Hills and the land of Tethyr. the seat of Emir of Tethyr was created by the Cajaan Dynasty, establishing it as a traditional post for the syl-pasha's lesser sons.

These emirs governed largely as they pleased, so as long as their subjects obeyed the qysar's dictates, paid their taxes, and supplied troops for the imperial armies as needed. the title of emir was supplanted by "satrap", being bestowed upon the provincial rulers of the Tashalar, Thindol, the cities of Lapaliiya, and the major settlements in the vast plains of Shaar, as far east as the Landrise.

Genie Titles

Among the genie races, emir was one of many common titles held by noble djinn and noble efreet, who served the Great Caliph of the Djinn They often commanded the mighty warmonger genies.

Among the djinn, emirs could be addressed at court by the additional titles of "the Imponderable" or "Worthy of the Heavens."

Emirs were a rank above beys in efreet society. The best of beys, or "Lord of the Black Flames", would be promoted to the station of emir if they did well on a field of battle. And whenever the Sultan of the Efreet visited the Prime Material plane, a single emir had to accompany his military escort. The efreet emirs viewed these two services to the Sultan as a necessary burden for the good of their whole race. However, they kept their thurgur strictly functional and spartanly military, affording the people under their command few comforts.

On the Prime Material plane, jann tribes were commonly ruled by amirs, such as the Jann of the Haunted Lands, the Jann of the High Desert, and the Lost City of Ubar. Whether they ruled a city or a mere wandering tribe, jann culture dictated that an amir always lived in "Resplendent Magnificence."

Additionally, the Great Padisha of the Marid was sometimes referred to as "Emir of All Currents". Marids of all sorts laid claim to titles such as "emir", though as they had no set order to their various self-professed rankings marid society was plagued by a hodgepodge of conflicting hierarchies and precedents.

Taangan Titles

In the Hordelands, prior to the 15 century DR, the title "emir" could be found in use in the nations of Murghôm and Semphar.

In both nations the title was used to refer to those appointed to act as a governor of a city or village. In the 14 century DR, the emir of Delbyl was served by sheriffs known as maliks (chiefs), who in turn commanded the civil watch. Emir Abi Talid in Semphar's Iliphanar was granted a far larger number of troops, a garrison of 500, as well as his own spy network in order to protect the Silk Road from bandits. Whilst Abui Fiydah, Emir of Estanil, was granted little authority, unable to defy the will of the Semphari town's Council of Loggers. And Murghôm's Tarman the Old, Emir of Phannaskul, was a demented man of old age who did little for the city and allowed the merchants to run amuck. From these examples one could conclude that the powers of the emir varied on a case by case basis.

The emirs of Murghôm were appointed to their posts by the Bey of Murghôm, whilst those of Semphar were appointed by the nation's Caliph.

Zakharan Titles

In the vast lands of Zakhara, "emir" was a common monarchical title used for either the reigning monarch of a sovereign principality, roughly translating to "lord", though this only occurred whenever there were no other available landed titles.

Zakhara's use of emir as a monarchical title dated back long before the discovery of the Law of the Loregiver and the founding of the Enlightened Throne. Under the Enlightened Throne they were a step below the Grand Caliph, granted parcels of land by him to rule over some of its many city-states and small empires. Emirs had the power to provide grants to still lesser magistrates, such as qadis, or to individual farmers.

Some of the emirates of Zakhara included Dihliz, Muluk of the Free Cities, and Qudra of the Pearl Cities. The emirs of Dihliz were always appointed by the Grand Caliph, at the recommendation of the padishah of Afyal. The emirs of Jumlat were some sort of ruling council, beneath the rule of its sultan. The emirs of Qudra were uniquely elected by mamluks among the city-state's mamluk societies, a tradition that began after they had overthrown its evil emir and selected one of their bravest leaders to take charge.

Among the cities of the Pantheist League, emir was a unique title, held by only one individual. This emir was the dominant force on all matters religious and secular for the Pantheists, being the head of the League Conclave, his authority rivaled only by that of the Grand Caliph. He and his fellow moralists held court at the capital city of I'tiraf.

Trivia

  • Crimmor, a city in Amn's region of Zehoarastria, had a tavern by the name of Emir's Court, harkening back to its past as an emir-ruled territory under the Calimshan Empire.
