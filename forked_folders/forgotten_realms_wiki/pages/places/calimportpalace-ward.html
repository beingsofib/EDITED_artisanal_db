Palace Ward was the foremost upper-class district of Calimport, the richest of all wards, and was one of the city's seven Central Wards.

Location

This area was located in the western center of the city, sandwiched between Jewel Ward to the east and Shackles Ward to the west. More specifically, it abutted Qhibal Sabban, the most prosperous and clean sector of Shackles Ward. To the north it was bordered by the Faiths Ward. And to the south it bordered the Armada and Dock Wards.

History

The area was formerly part of the Temple and Learning Quarter, before Syl-Pasha Ralan el Pesarkhal established the system of wards in the , superseding the long-standing system of sabbans and drudachs. greater areas of mobility, as well as to keep the latter out of sight.

In 1092 DR, Palace Ward's Darehj Sabban was one of many affected by the blaze later known as Akham's Duel.

In the , the Syl-Pasha broke ground on a long-term construction project, a defensive barrier he dubbed the Pesarkhal Wall. By the next year it encompassed the Armada, Palace, and Shackles Wards. El Pesarkhal planned for it to eventually enclose the entirety of the Central Wards.

Government

In the late 14th century DR, the Sultan of Palace Ward was Tomuk yn Jhiavor el Kahmir. Though he was a daft, ineffectual, and paranoid sultan he managed to maintain his position on account of being the second grandson of Nur el Kahmir, the Pasha of Manshaka, another city in Calimshan that was allied to El Pesarkhal.

Inhabitants

The upper streets of Palace Ward were home to some of the most affluent and richest people in Calimshan, as well as a great deal of the pashas, Many of these residents were of the "old money" variety, being inheritors of their considerable wealth.

The lower level of the ward, out of sight of the upper classes, was reserved for their servants and slaves. Life here largely operated as any other part of Calimport, but due to its obscured nature many sultans, pashas, and the like conducted clandestine activities in the understreets with the city's criminal powers.

Sabbans

Palace Ward was largely a residential ward, with much of its space occupied by the villas of pashas, often only being broken up by the likes of row houses, taverns, and the garrisons for hired guards and mercenaries. However, much like its neighbors, the distinctions between these sabbans and drudachs was blurred due to its layered infrastructure.

The slightly vaulted, stonework secondary layer of Palace Ward was the "ground level" to its rich denizens. Braced by both stone and timber supports, it was comprised of solid street avenues and courts, granting the upper classes more uninterrupted movement than the sabban-wall byways afforded. The engineers responsible constructed it in such a manner as to ensure that it would stay elevated even if half of these timber supports were broken. One could access the city's true ground level and vice-versa by traversing sets of stairs.

The edges of the ward's byways, served as the "curbs" of this layer. As the boundary defining sabban marks were not present on the second layer, those that walked its elevated streets used the glazed coloration of these curbs to determine which sabban and drudach they were standing in. Each were assigned a distinct coloration, with the latter being denoted as a stripe at the base of the sabban color.

The lower level of Palace Ward was often lumped together with those of the Grand and Jewel wards as the "Upper Muzad", being considered the uppermost layer of the Muzhahajaarnadah. In some areas there were even said to be hidden entrances to the Muzad. With the sky blocked out by the elevated streets, save for stairwells and the gates to the open-air Outer or Port Wards, torches had to be relied upon for illumination.

Darehj Sabban

Also known as the "Mosaic Sabban", due to the myriad of inlaid mosaics that adorned its upraised street sections and many buildings. It was one of two sabbans in Palace Ward that lied along the Pesarkhal Wall. And as it abutted the Armada and Shackles Wards, its business and residents were heavily involved in the sea and slave trades.

The sabban mark of Darehj depicted a straight line of five chain links. The color displayed on the edges of its walls was scarlet red, whilst the colors at their base representing its drudachs were black, white, and yellow. The three drudachs that made up the sabban were Adhaef Drudach, Borto Drudach, and Dehk Drudach.

Khatrev Sabban

Also known as the "Jhambiya Sabban", a disparaging nickname born from a long reputation as a site of assassinations. This northernmost sector offered the easiest ingress to the Pasha's Sabban.

It was one of the two sabbans in Palace Ward that bordered the Pesarkhal Wall. The gatehouses between that outer wall and Khatrev Sabban's own inner walls were not heavily guarded, for most guards were instead stationed at the area of highest traffic, the sabban's conenction to Faiths Ward.

The sabban mark of Khatrev was a crude depiction of a jambiya, consisting of a curled slash with a perpendicular slash across its upper end. The color displayed on the edges of its walls was black, whilst the colors at their base representing its drudachs were green, sky blue, and turquoise. The three drudachs that made up the sabban were Nar'asr Drudach, Qadeem Drudach, and Sra'ala Drudach.

Najlet Sabban

Also known by the monikers "Demonshadow Sabban" and "The Martyr's Sabban", in remembrance of a significant event that occurred in the sabban back during the Eighth Age of Calimshan. In the , a rival for the throne conjured a rogue tanar'ri by the name of Chanaavokh. It rampaged across the streets of Calimport, killing all who crossed its path, until over 200 laid slain.

The then-Syl Pasha's second son, Ikham yn Herak el Najle, confronted the vile fiend amid a ruined courtyard and the rubble of two buildings. He fought Chanaavokh to a stand still, their battle eventually ending in their mutual demise. As their blood intermingled, the stone upon which they fell was stained dark red. Ever since that day, a dark silhouette of the two was present, and when upperstreets were constructed a dark outline appeared on the stone street directly overhead of the original site.

Najlet Sabban's mark reflected this story and the strange stain it left behind on the sabban, consisting of a silhouetted head with greatly exaggerated horns on its sides. The color displayed on the edges of its walls was royal blue, whilst the colors at their base representing its drudachs were golden rod yellow, ivory, and kelly green. The four drudachs that made up the sabban were Calimra Drudach, Ilhif Drudach, Tistad Drudach, and Vina'atf Drudach.
