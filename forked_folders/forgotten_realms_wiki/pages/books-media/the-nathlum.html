The Nathlum was an infamous book of spells and poison recipes compiled sometime in or before the 10 century DR.

Description

The book had a gray canvas cover into which was sewn curling brown parchment pages. Although nondescript in appearance, the book was protected by immensely powerful and evil magic. Any good person who held The Nathlum suffered constant burning pain in their head and blinding pain in their eyes, which was intense enough to kill them if they did not relinquish the book. This magic was resistant to any normal attempt to dispel it.

Contents

The Nathlum detailed recipes for sixteen potent poisons, most notably belpren, dwarfbane, huld, jeteye, lhurdas, orvas, prespra, ulcrun, and varrakas.

It also contained four unique glyphs of warding, each of which could only be cast by an evil cleric and could only harm those who were good. These glyphs were known as curtal, hlack, yammas, and zuth.

The tome was also known to contain the formula for the maze spell.

History

Both the meaning of the name and the true origins of The Nathlum were unknown.

The first presumed record of The Nathlum was made by the sage Orfidel, who described a tome matching its description in the possession of the evil mage Lethchauntos sometime prior to the Orcfastings War of the .

During the Orcfastings War, the book was claimed by Tymor Threeshields as part of his spoils, and the first official record of the tome's existence appeared in his accountings. It was believed that he was eager to rid himself of the book, and subsequently sent The Nathlum to Neverwinter as part of a dowry for his daughter, Nulauznee. The book then vanished from records.

It was not until the mid–14 century DR that The Nathlum reappeared, still seemingly complete, when it came into the possession of the wizard Phrandjas in Port Llast. Despite the great pain that reading the tome inflicted upon him, Phrandjas persevered in reading and making notes about the book, which he shared with the likes of Elminster (who would go on to disseminate knowledge about how to recognize some of the poisons and glyphs). Phrandjas also copied the recipes for the tome's various poisons with the intention of selling them to local alchemists, although it was unclear if he had shared them as of the mid-to-late 14 century DR.
