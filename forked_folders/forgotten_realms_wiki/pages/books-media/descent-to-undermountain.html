Descent to Undermountain (also known simply as Undermountain) is a role-playing game created and distributed by in 1998. It was developed by Chris Avellone, Scott Bennie, John Deiley, , Steve Perrin.

Story

In Eleint of 1370 DR, a hero was summoned by Khelben Arunsun at the behest of the Lords of Waterdeep to investigate the recent increase in kobold activity and thefts across Waterdeep. The Blackstaff handed the future hero two gold coins to pay for the passage to Undermountain through the Yawning Portal and sent him on his way.

The hero uncovered the reason behind the kobolds' attacks: a drow High Priestess of Lolth, Azurna, was using the kobolds as part of her alliance to recover the ancient artifact that belonged to Lolth and bring ruin to Waterdeep. Continuing his investigation, the hero visited various locations within Undermountain and confronted Azurna's allies while collecting the pieces of a mysterious stone spider.

The Khelbenite confronted a Lolth-worshiping undead necromancer known as the Ghoulmaster in the Cassalanter family tomb on the Dungeon Level of Undermountain. He disbanded the operations of the sinister Shadow Thieves after their well-hidden return to Waterdeep under the leadership of the mysterious Xabash. He retrieved a lost heirloom that belonged to Maskar Wands from the Tomb of Ankh-Kephra, a Mulhorandi wizard. Later, he stormed an ancient dwarvish' prison taken over by the fiendish orc Horazak and his marauders. After that, he retrieved the Black Wand of Myrkul, aiding the Blackstaff's personal vendetta against the dead deity, and avenged the dwarves of the Clangeddin's Hearth, who had been slaughtered by Azurna's drow forces. The hero defeated the undead servants of Morik Stormhand, an old lich allied with the drow, as well as his fellow worshipers of Velsharoon.

Eventually, the search for the spider pieces led to a confrontation with one of Halaster Blackcloak's apprentices, Ibbalar Thrul, who unleashed an army of spectators on the City of Splendors. Stopping the assault, the Khelbenite gained for himself the gratitude of both the city of Waterdeep as well as several noble families, such as Cassalanter, Wands, Phaulkon, Moonstar, and Ruldegost.

Eventually, Lolth revealed the Khelbenite's search for the spider statue pieces to her priestess, Matron Azurna, and also gave her the location of the vault containing the Flame Sword of Lolth. The hero, who had gathered all the pieces of the statue, brought them to the Flame Sword's vault and re-assembled the statue. This gave the Spider Goddess access to the Prime Material plane via the Sword's divine powers. Taking the form of a huge female drider as her avatar, Lolth fought the Khelbenite. A fierce battle ensued, but the avatar was ultimately defeated. The hero destroyed the stone spider, banishing the avatar and making the Flame Sword disappear in an explosion of arcane magic. Waterdeep was safe once again, with the remains of Azurna's alliance destroyed or dispersed.

Gameplay

The game partially derives its title from the use of the 3D graphical engine. Undermountain allowed the player to interact with NPCs, monsters, and the general environment from a first-person perspective. The quest took place within the environs of the infamous super-dungeon of the Undermountain. Real-time combat would mix with puzzles to provide a variety of challenges throughout the vast dungeon.

The game was not the first to bring PC role-playing into a 3D environment, having been preceded by several titles such as ' Elder Scrolls Arena and Elder Scrolls Daggerfall along with 's Ultima Underworld series. It was, however, noted for being the first RPG to use a dedicated 3D engine such as that behind '' to create a 3D world based on the Advanced Dungeons & Dragons'' license.

As the back of the box stated, the game included six different races that could take on single or multi-class professions with a variety of unique abilities such as thieves being able to climb walls. Over fifty different 3D monsters were in the game along with 160 magical items and forty different spells.

Reception

Players and critics alike have noted the unfinished feel of the game upon release. Cooperative multiplayer support, previously promised as a part of the title, was cut late in development because of severe technical issues to focus on the single player campaign. The decision to use the Descent 1 engine was also cited as a design issue, as it required heavy rewrites to the code in order to support an RPG setting such as Undermountain. Bugs, embarrassingly weak AI, the unappealing and shoddy nature of the graphics, and several other issues have attributed to a general consensus of the game as an example of a title that was pushed to release before it was ready.
