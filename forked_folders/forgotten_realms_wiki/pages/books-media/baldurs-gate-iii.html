Baldur's Gate III is a computer game released by and is the third main game in the Baldur's Gate series. It is based on a highly modified version of the 5-edition Dungeons & Dragons rule set and is set after the Baldur's Gate: Descent into Avernus tabletop adventure. It was first released as an early access game, after which other races, classes, features, and the rest of the story was added.

Gameplay

Baldur's Gate III is a role-playing video game that features both single-player and cooperative multiplayer modes. It allows players to generate one or more characters and assemble a party, which can include pre-generated characters provided by the game, to engage with the narrative. Additionally, the game offers the option for players to use their characters in online cooperative play, collaborating with others to form a party. The game takes characters from levels 1 to 12.

The game employs a constrained free-floating camera system, enabling gameplay through various perspectives, from a complete third-person viewpoint to an isometric top-down angle. Its user interface supports input via both mouse and keyboard as well as controller configurations. Across these interfaces, players have the capacity to execute spells and combat maneuvers, oversee inventory, access the map, view statistics of both player and companion characters, and interact with numerous gameplay components, including allies and adversaries.

The majority of the game's mechanics and spells originate from the Dungeons & Dragons. However, modifications and omissions have been made to accommodate the transition to a role-playing video game format.

The game provides support for modifications. A wide range of mods, developed by the community, are accessible, enabling players to alter numerous facets of the game.

Characters

The player creates their character, including their race and class from the Player's Handbook. The game comprises 12 character classes, each further divided into 46 subclasses, with each class emphasizing a distinct facet of the combat system. Additionally, the game facilitates the selection of multiple classes for a single character through a feature known as multiclassing, enabling players to develop their characters in diverse and unique manners. The ranger class is largely modified, and some subclasses are essentially new, such as the Wildheart barbarian subclass that replaces the Totem Warrior.

The character also chooses a background from the standard 5-edition list, their ability scores, and skills they are proficient in. Players also have the option to craft a character from the beginning, select from six pre-designed "Origins" character, or opt for a customizable seventh origin character termed The Dark Urge. If an "Origins" character is not selected, they may still be available as a party member. The game has a tutorial system to introduce the players to the basic mechanics.

Jaheira and Minsc are characters who made appearances in both Baldur's Gate and its sequel, Baldur's Gate II: Shadows of Amn.

The game includes a selection of ten companion characters that players can recruit into their party. Each companion character possesses a unique personal story and narrative arc for the player to investigate. Players have the opportunity to cultivate relationships with these companions, influenced by decisions in the game's plot and dialogues. Access to certain companion characters is contingent upon making specific choices within the game's narrative and dialogue options.

Every character in the game, regardless of their significance, is endowed with full voice acting and motion capture, collectively contributing to approximately 1.5 million words of performance capture. The game is fully narrated by voice actress Amelia Tyler, who acts as the Dungeon Master. In this capacity, she articulates the setting and narrates the player's endeavors throughout the narrative.

Quests

The game features an adaptable quest system, enabling multiple methodologies for quest resolution. It permits the removal of nearly any non-player character, irrespective of their relevance to the narrative, without impeding game progression. Structured into three acts, each set within a unique geographical area, the game employs a non-linear approach, allowing quests to be undertaken in an arbitrary order.

Combat

Diverging from its predecessors in the Baldur's Gate series, the game introduces turn-based combat, with all combat encounters governed by the rules of Dungeons & Dragons 5 Edition.

In combat, the player can interact with the environment with standard actions (as well as bonus actions), such as attack, hide, or shoving a creature. Many other Dungeons & Dragons systems are implemented, such as rolling for initiative and death saving throws, imposing advantage or disadvantage, and being in difficult terrain. When combat has taken its toll, the player can make camp, which expands over time as more companions are recruited.

Setting

Baldur's Gate III is set in the year 1492 DR, marking a period over 120 years after the events depicted in Baldur's Gate II: Shadows of Amn and several months following the occurrences within Baldur's Gate: Descent into Avernus. The narrative primarily takes place along the Sword Coast in western Faerûn, featuring a wooded region that houses the Emerald Grove, a druidic sanctuary devoted to the deity Silvanus; Moonrise Towers and the Shadow-cursed Lands, areas engulfed by an unnatural, sentient darkness that necessitates magical intervention for navigation; and Baldur's Gate, the region's most populous and prosperous city, including its neighboring suburb of Rivington. The journey also extends to other realms such as the Underdark, the Astral Plane, and Avernus.

Plot

The player character starts off on a nautiloid. Along with their fellow prisoners, the character has been confined within a holding pod, and a mind flayer tadpole has been inserted into their eye. The spelljammer makes its way across Faerûn, and begins to destroy buildings as it flies across Yartar. A group of red dragon-riding githyanki intercept and attack it, and the dragons start to gain the advantage. The mind flayer then commands the nautiloid to seemingly disappear, and it spawns in a different location, and courses through Hell. The githyanki, however, manage to track down the spelljammer, and attack it once more. Finding an opening in the damaged vessel, a red dragon breathes fire over a vulnerable spot, causing an explosion that throws the illithid off balance. The mind flayer tries to relocate the nautiloid yet another time, but the explosion causes it to become very badly damaged. Teleporting back to Faerûn, the nautiloid crashes to the ground.

The player character awakes, stranded close to the bank of River Chionthar (somewhere seemingly very close to Elturel), where the rubble of the spelljammer covers the ground. They find themselves with new abilities, including telepathy. The player adventures on and finds companions and followers along the way (some of which were also alongside them on the nautiloid). However, their potential companions believe that ceremorphosis will soon take hold, and they seek out a powerful cleric to heal them.

In their quest for a remedy, the group exhausts multiple strategies, none of which yield a solution to their predicament. A fortuitous encounter with a "Dream Visitor," harbored within a prism possessed by Shadowheart, provides temporary respite from the larvae's dominating psychic influence. This prism, coveted by the githyanki, becomes a focal point in the narrative. The player character and their companions then find themselves entangled in the destinies of the Emerald Grove, a sanctuary contested by local druids, tiefling refugees, and a goblin horde dedicated to a cult known as the "Absolute." The group's decisions significantly impact the grove's fate before they embark towards Moonrise Towers, a bastion of the cult, in pursuit of a lasting cure.

Upon reaching the vicinity of Moonrise Towers, the group seek refuge in the Last Light Inn, which serves as a haven for a diverse group including tiefling refugees, members of the Flaming Fist, and the Harpers. The narrative reveals that General Ketheric Thorm, the sovereign of Moonrise and an acolyte of Myrkul, manipulates a relic known as the "Nightsong" to sustain his immortality. The protagonists delve into the depths of Moonrise, uncovering that the Nightsong is in fact Aylin, a descendant of Selûne. The confrontation with Ketheric, alongside his allies Orin the Red and Enver Gortash—devotees of the deities Bhaal and Bane, respectively—culminates in a revelation that an elder brain, representing the pinnacle of illithid evolution, seeks dominion over the Sword Coast with the trio's assistance. The protagonist thwarts Ketheric, seizing control of his Netherstone.

The narrative shifts to Baldur's Gate, where the protagonist navigates the manipulations of Gortash and Orin, while their companions resolve their individual quests. The "Dream Visitor" is disclosed to be an illithid dissenter known as The Emperor, who utilizes the prism to contain a formidable githyanki named Orpheus, whose abilities counteract the Absolute's influence. The climax involves a confrontation with Gortash and Orin, during which the protagonist utilizes the Netherstones to challenge the Elder Brain. Despite the Elder Brain's anticipation of these events, the Emperor intervenes, turning the tide.

The resolution hinges on the protagonist's decision to either liberate Orpheus, allowing for his transformation and causing the Emperor to assimilate with the Absolute, or to have the Emperor, Karlach in an altered state, or the protagonist themselves consume Orpheus to fulfill their mission. The final confrontation with the Elder Brain presents a choice: annihilate it and all illithid larvae, eradicating the threat permanently, or betray their allies to ascend as the new Absolute.
