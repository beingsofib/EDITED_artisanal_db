The Rundeen, originally meaning the Safe Coin and also known as the Tradesmen or the Trader's Friend, was an expansive and dominating mercantile, criminal, and slaving consortium and secret society that operated in Calimshan, Tashalar, the Chultan Peninsula, and the southern Sword Coast.

Organization

Structure

The Rundeen had a relatively loose and informal hierarchy, but had a clear, layered structure. These layers operated independently and even unaware of the others, with only the leadership grasping the full picture of the organization.

The leaders of the Rundeen were known as the Yrshelem, an Alzhedo word meaning 'coin collector(s).' They were the secret inner circle of the organization, never identified nor directly observed and directing affairs covertly. The Yrshelem were in turn divided into two tiers; there were the five Grand Yrshelem and under them were the fifteen First Yrshelem. Overall, the Yrshelem operated as an oligarchy. The members of the Grand Yrshelem each had an area of specialty and authority, controlling a particular aspect of Rundeen business—caravans, raw goods, money-changing and loans, shipping, and slavery—but evenly divided both the responsibilities and the profits, aiding one another when it came necessary. Although Calishite society had traditionally sexist attitudes against women outside the home, let alone in positions of power, the Grand Yrshelem had included many female members over the centuries. Known members of the Grand Yrshelem circa 1370 DR were:

  • The Grand Yrshelem of Ships: Zuras el Ireen
  • The Grand Yrshelem of Caravans: Rafi al Shuree
  • The Grand Yrshelem of Slaves: Saibhon el Bhiras

Next, the members of the First Yrshelem mainly handled the organization and management of the Rundeen's assets, holdings, personnel, and finances. Each of the Grand Yrshelem was served by a triad of First Yrshelem: the first to handle money, the second personnel, and the third physical assets and costs, divided within their superior's area of authority.

Serving the First Yrshelem was a group known as the Mitalibbar, meaning 'wealth watchers.' Each mitalib—a word meaning 'watcher'—was an agent tasked with collecting membership fees, making payments, and communicating with merchants, as well as looking out for rivals, creditors, and enemies like the Harpers or Shadow Thieves, in his or her city, for which tasks they would employ agents of their own. Broadly, they were the Rundeen's collection agency. While the Yrshelem directed the overall course of the Rundeen through the Mitalibbar, they permitted them autonomy in their day-to-day affairs. Within themselves, the Mitalibbar had ranks named for and in order of importance of the value of old coins from at first ancient Tashalar and later the Shoon Imperium—the highest ranking mitalib were called nirdeen (singular: nirdey; platinum coins), those of the next rank were tardeen (singular: tardey; electrum coins), and the those of the lowest rank were cardeen (singular: cardey; copper coins). Each nirdeen served one of the First Yrshelem triads according to their financial power in a given city, though some nirdeen were superior to others and all would obey any member of the Yrshelem. All merchants and lesser members paid their fees to their local nirdey, who was often the highest-level Tradesman they knew of; they rarely even saw anyone higher. The Mitalibbar had cells in every city in the Rundeen's domain and a nirdey or tardey agent in every port or caravan city south of Lake Weng in Amn as of 1370 DR. In the cities of Calimshan, with their power and influence in the underworld, a nirdey had the effective status of a pasha, albeit with superiors of their own, and some Calishite pashas either secretly served the Mitalibbar or even were members of the Mitalibbar, which erased many distinctions between them. Known members of the Mitalibbar circa 1370 DR included:

  • Husuf yn Harun el Halid, nirdey of the Emerald Ward of Calimport
  • Jafar el Uabar, nirdey of Volothamp
  • Daudon yn Saibh el Yakan, nirdey of Memnon

Below the Mitalibbar, but serving the First Yrshelem directly, were a variety of subsidiary organizations and operatives that supported Rundeen goals or enacted its commands. These included noble houses with historical alliances with the Rundeen, like the Vyndahla clan of Calimshan; mercenary and adventuring companies that worked as enforcers, private armies, and treasure hunters such as the Clenched Fist; top assassins like the Fist of Rundeen; pirate fleets including the Bloody Buccaneers, the Scimitar of Fire, and the Sea Reavers; and thieves groups like the Many Long Arms, as well as a host of other semi-independent enforcers, smugglers, and thieves.

Lastly, at the bottom of the pile came numerous regular Tradesmen, both individuals and their families. These folk were ordinary merchants, caravaneers, moneylenders, shipbuilders, and other business owners. They had no titles nor hardly any influence in the Rundeen. It would take many years of labor or significant profits for them to be promoted.

Recruitment

Being an exclusive organization, the Rundeen did not accept any common merchant. When considering new members, the Rundeen took an interest in merchants and organizations with links to an industry in which they specialized, such as shipping, caravaning, or trade, and who had businesses big enough to warrant their attention. For example, a small-time moneylender of Schamedar who had clients in neighboring Manshaka was of no interest, but one who reached across Calimshan to Memnon or even into Tethyr at Myratma had both the coin and the ambition to catch their eye. Thus, a prospective merchant must also be competent, cunning, efficient, and prosperous. When a worthy merchant satisfying all three measures was identified by an agent, information on them and their business was reported up through the Mitalibbar and Yrshelem to the Grand Yrshelem who oversaw their particular trade. That Grand Yrshelem would decide whether to decline or offer membership. In the latter case, a Rundeen representative would contact the merchant to offer a contract and incentives to join, not threats and fearmongering. They did not need to. Those who rejected the offer were either approached again later until they did, or else treated as a rival and driven out of business, or even killed or disappeared if particularly opposed, to make way for an existing Rundeen member. After seeing the fate of their fellows, many businesses were quite careful when consider declining the Rundeen. Those who accepted were counted as business partners of the Rundeen.

In contrast, bandits, pirates, and other criminals hired by the Rundeen were required to demonstrate discipline, loyalty, restraint, and the ability to follow orders exactly, doing no more and no less than required. For example, if a pirate vessel was ordered to only pursue a rival merchant ship, it should not instead board and raid that ship, while one ordered to board a ship must not sink it. Laxity, recklessness, and carelessness would harm the Rundeen's reputation. Meanwhile, adventurers should not be over concerned about doing what was right, legal, or proper.

Benefits

Partners of the Rundeen enjoyed an array of benefits. First, their caravans received greater protection against bandits and their ships against pirates—often the Rundeen's own—discount rates on various services and products purchased from Rundeen merchants or their allies, preferential treatment in any port or city under Rundeen mercantile control, and more general benefits from being a fellow Tradesman. The reduced prices on wagons and ships alone was sufficient to make membership worthwhile for any enterprising merchant.

Rundeen control of markets brought greater benefits. For example, in Almraiven circa 1370, half of all shipbuilders (including Yulthaari Shipyards) were either sponsored or owned by the Rundeen, which granted them cheaper rates on lumber and labor from Lapaliiya, the Tashalar, and the Shaar, while Rundeen ships were prioritized for construction, sale, and outfitting—even Calimshan's syl-pasha came second, but was usually unaware of it. Meanwhile, in both Almraiven and Schamedar, the masters of the coopers', farriers', moneylenders', saddlers', and shipwrights' guilds were all full members of the Rundeen, which paid their fees, taxes, and personal bribes to local governments on their behalf. This in turn established closer links with vizars who in granted Rundeen members best rates, business opportunities, and patronage.

Terms & Conditions

The requirements of membership in the Rundeen were simple, yet deceptive. First, a prospective member must sign a contract agreeing to pay dues worth 5% of their monthly earnings and this collector counted, tallied, and distributed the coin as required by superiors. But these memberships dues were a quick and rocky road to ruin.

First, as a matter of courtesy, a partner should come to their mitalib at the appointed time with the proper amount—making the Tradesmen ask for their money was seen as rude. If the partner did not visit the mitalib, then a cardey came to them with a friendly reminder of their responsibilities. The partner must then visit the nirdey with their payment by dusk the next day; once this was done, the matter was resolved without disgrace. But if the partner failed to show in time, then a gang of enforcers came around to their home to break a few things as a warning. If they still failed to pay after a tenday, then the enforcers returned and this time cut off the partner's finger. Otherwise, if the paid amount was less than required, the partner was given only a tenday's grace in which to make up the shortfall.

In either case, the Yrshelem were notified the partner had defaulted and the partner was informed of their updated payment plan. Alternatively, the Rundeen could choose to take up to 70% of earnings and retain the former owner as an employed manager instead), and the Gold Lion Wheelwrights of Saelmur).

If at any point the debtor ran away rather than pay up, then the Rundeen sent its enforcers to track them down. Their extensive connections and transport businesses made it hard to escape them for long; most were apprehended in only a few tendays. They had a grim fate: their tongues were cut out to make them mute and they were sold as slaves.

Membership

The Rundeen was a vast organization, with approximately 3,000 standard members and countless more people employed by them or otherwise involved.

They were largely merchants, pirates, bandits, and others, each a specialist in their respective areas and operating mostly independently, especially at the Rundeen's lower levels. As such, they shared little in the way or training or talents. Nevertheless, some skills were useful, especially to those canny enough to hone them. Caravaneers did well to practice tracking and orienteering, while wise Rundeen pirates learned matters of ship design, livery, heraldry, and appraisal to target the correct ships and cargos. Those who'd been in the Rundeen for several months acquired a sense of etiquette useful in identifying fellow Tradesmen and their superiors and not inadvertently slighting them.

Meanwhile, those in the higher, leadership positions no longer needed to do much work, especially physical work. They needed only employ their sharp business acumen and knowledge of trade to advance.

Activities

Goals & Strategy

The Tradesmen purported to stand for the common benefit of merchants everywhere who had to travel and brave dangers—that was merely a byproduct of controlling trade and was too much effort besides. The Rundeen operated as a, by and large, legitimate business network, despite their domineering practices. They preferred to induce potential rivals to join them than to threaten them, and applied brute force against rivals and potentials threats only as necessary. Moreover, in contrast to mercantile social groups that created trade alliances like the Knights of the Shield, the Rundeen were an independent organization with its own resources and finances: they controlled fleets, shipyards, costers, caravans, and associated businesses, and operated unseen in every area of trade in the South, and few but the leaders of great realms like Calimshan could check their power.

In contrast to other mercantile organizations of their kind, the Rundeen were disinterested in production and trade goods and instead focused on the trade itself: the routes, services, maintenance, ships, warehouses, and so on. They had their hands in the businesses and guilds of carters, coachmen, and dockworkers; the farriers and trainers of the horses that led caravans; the makers of harnesses and saddles for animals; both shipwrights and wagonwrights; the sailors and officers of trade ships; moneychangers and moneylenders; coopers who made barrels and joiners who made crates for transporting goods; the butchers, fishermen, and fishmongers who turned livestock and fish into meat for sale; and the salters and packers who preserved and prepared food for trade; and even folk who carved wood and ivory. What trade goods they did deal in directly were raw materials like wood, grains, spices, sugar, wool in its raw form, furs, and leather that was tanned but not yet worked. They also traded in slaves.

Thanks to their ruthless dominance and support and protection of members reducing hazards, Rundeen businesses were historically able to embark on riskier and longer-range trade expeditions and business ventures that would earn greater profits, as compared to regular trading costers. Moreover, they could reserve the longest, riskiest, and most profitable trade routes for themselves, and leave the local scraps to lesser merchants. In addition, they had contacts throughout Faerûn, which aided in finding markets in far-off lands. Thus, for example, they could arrange trade from Calimshan to as far away as Luiren in the Shining South or the ports around the Inner Sea, thereby accessing goods and markets that most other costers of the Shining Sea could not hope to reach, and do so regularly and with high profits. Rundeen caravans were protected by well-armed guards.

To support their efforts, the Rundeen dedicated a great deal of time and attention to simply monitoring the trade landscape: keeping an eye on the roads and their caravans to keep them safe; watching the seas for pirates and rival vessels; checking out markets for popular goods; identifying merchants and others that might make worthy members and partners; determining what cargos or important persons rivals were transporting; and investigating lesser Rundeen members for double-dealing. For example, the Brothers Humuz stone carvers of Calimport spent time working in wealthy clients' homes and reported what they saw and heard to their Rundeen masters every sixmonth. Despite this, they moved slowly, preferring to consider all aspects before deciding on a course. They would only act swiftly when needed to protect a partner from banditry or piracy, or to strike at a rival with their own banditry or piracy.

Naturally, many members venerated Waukeen, the Merchant's Friend, but any faith might be found amongst them.

Piracy & Banditry

Piracy was a traditional preferred tactic of the Rundeen, not merely to take treasure but as a way to sabotage rivals' businesses and reinforce their own control of sea trade. They employed bandits in much the same way on land against caravans. Against rival merchants who were either trying to crack into a Rundeen-monopolized market or who elected not to use Rundeen services, the pirates or bandits disrupted their shipping or caravans or scared them away, pushing them toward ruin. They could also steal trade secrets for the Rundeen and interfere with others' trade deals. The Three Scourges pirates would even steal cargoes, pose as the actual crew of the captured vessels, and brazenly deliver the goods themselves to take the profits for the Rundeen.

Through messages (which might be ordinary or via magic), the Rundeen would inform their employed pirates or bandits of the routes, destinations, and current positions of specific ships or caravans that were worth their effort. They would also give them precise instructions for the raid: whether or not to only harry or actually sink a ship; whether to damage, destroy, or steal cargo; and whether or not to harm personnel and passengers. Pirates might be ordered to pursue a vessel but not board it, or board it but not scuttle it. The Rundeen expected such missions to be carried out to the letter and never exceeded; otherwise, they punish the pirates. Should a ship escape the pirates, which may or may not be intended, the owners would be force to consider going elsewhere or joining the Rundeen.

Known Rundeen pirates were the Bloody Buccaneers, the Scimitar of Fire, the Sea Reavers, and the Three Scourges, Groups that engaged in banditry included the Clenched Fist.

Such piracy was previously commonplace on the Lake of Steam, but in the late 1360s DR it was only an occasional problem around the Nelanther Isles. Meanwhile, the Rundeen had long wielded their influence to prevent Tashalar, Calimshan, and Lapaliiya from sending their navies against the pirate haven of Narubel in Thindol.

Slavery

In the face of opposition from antislavery organizations, the Rundeen continued to engage in slavery as a sideline, even keeping the despicable practice going long after it would've been disrupted and abandoned for being unprofitable if nothing else. They made themselves the key link in the slave trade in the South, and brooked no opposition or competition to their dominance.

Rundeen slavers targeted coastal villages and inland communities in Chult, Tashalar, Thindol, and the Shaar, raiding these places and carrying away native peoples. Prominent Rundeen slaving companies included the Ice Chain and the Haedi family of Almraiven. Their slave trade was thriving around 1367 DR, but no longer a total monopoly as of 1370 DR.

Unsurprisingly, Rundeen slavers prided themselves on their professionalism and dismissed smaller competitors as "amateurs". A starved, sickly, or injured slave meant lower profits at auction and a dead one none at all, so they made sure to provide sufficient water, food, cold baths, clean-enough clothing, space to sleep in the hold, and otherwise took good care of their living cargo. Standing-room-only holds, beatings, whippings, mutilation, and rape were not reported in the Rundeen slave trade.

Ironically, the Rundeen were also the primary opponents of slavery—by others. To maintain their grip, they gave their pirates standing orders to attack rival slave ships from their targeted regions, making it too dangerous for independent slavers and even Calimshan's navy, el Nallojal, to conduct their own raids. They would even give tip-offs about rival slavers and their plans to known Harpers or Janessar who would eliminate the competition for them.

Mercenaries, Adventurers, & Assassins

Finally, the Rundeen either employed or sponsored a number of mercenary and adventuring companies, and even a band of assassins.

Bases of Operations

While Tashalar was the old seat of Rundeen rule, much of their power was centered in Calimshan as of the late 1300s. The Yrshelem were based out of a variety of private residences in the cities of these two realms, including Calimport, Manshaka, Memnon, and Tashluta. Whenever they met in person, they chose a different one of these bases each time, to better keep their activities hidden and to throw off spies.

From here, the Tradesmen monopolized trade across much of southwestern Faerûn, especially the realms around the Shining Sea, and indeed on the ruling merchant oligarchy and their defensive navy. In Calimshan circa 1370 DR, their control was reduced to only certain areas of the realm or to greater or lesser degrees, owing to the rise of Syl-Pasha Ralan el Pesarkhal causing them to lose Calimport and Almraiven. Nevertheless, either directly or via their members, they still controlled almost half, over 40%, of the shipping and trade across Calimshan in a majority of relevant industries, as well as the majority of ports and caravans on the northern coast of the Chultan Peninsula. And while they were yet strong in Tashalar and Chult, the fewer cities and settlements here made them less appealing to the Tradesmen, so they looked north across the sea, especially after they'd become entrenched in Calimshan. They also had members to the east in the Shaar. They were also making inroads into Amn, Tethyr, and further north, thanks to their links with the Knights of the Shield. They sought to control trade up to the Dragon's Neck Peninsula.

To a lesser degree, the Rundeen had agents distributed all over the Shining South, in Lantan and Mintarn, Westgate on the Dragon Coast, and in every primary trading port in Faerûn where humans were predominant. Outside their core territory, these agents could not of course exert the Rundeen's desired control, but could acquire local goods as needed without undue difficulty.

Symbol

For their symbol, when they used one at all, the Rundeen favored a coin penetrated by a nail. This might be an actual object or it might be an image drawn or inscribed on a surface. This alluded to the meaning of their earliest name, the Safe Coin.

In addition, members identified and greeted one another by means of a secret gesture: crossing their wrists over their chests and then clenching their fists. (The Knights of the Shield had a similar custom, but slightly different: the Knights closed their fists before crossing their wrists, the Rundeen, after.) An informal variant was a simple clenched fist.

Possessions

Members wore no uniform or livery nor any other consistent elements. The only thing they had in common was the superiority of their gear: high-quality tools, wagons, and even ships, sold to them at a discount by Rundeen merchants or Rundeen-owned businesses. Moreover, many of the Rundeen enjoyed showing off their riches, often adorning themselves in jewelry, except when they might want to avoid attention.

Similarly, Rundeen ships and caravans bore no badges or colors that might indicate them.

Relationships

Allies

When it came to allies, the Rundeen could afford to stand alone—they were big enough, powerful enough, and entrenched enough to be able to maintain their power and satisfy their own needs. Nevertheless, they established a few as needed.

The first, and most fraught, relationship was with the Knights of the Shield. While the Calishites saw no issue with divided loyalties and goals, the Rundeen were a little put-off by it. Connections could also run through families: Rundeen moneylender Nurih al Suhal had a Knight of the Shield brother-in-law Roffas Calindor.

Less of an alliance, during the el Djenispool Dynasty the Rundeen enjoyed complete control of six of the dozen or more major criminal families of Calimshan. After the Darkstalker Wars and Syl-Pasha Ralan, however, only four remained by 1370 DR and all were under el Pesarkhal control. The only remaining Rundeen family was the Vyndahla clan of smugglers and pirates operating in Almraiven and Volothamp, who worked with the Rundeen to oust el Pesarkhal, who in turn waited for a chance to destroy them too.

The Twisted Rune had had some unspecified influence on the Rundeen over the course of its history, by means of cat's-paw agents and utter secrecy.

One of their more recent allies around 1370 DR was the Se'Sehen tribe of yuan-ti, who came to them to learn more of human organizations and trade. While the yuan-ti supplied exotic herbs and poisons from the jungles, the Rundeen delivered slaves to the snake people and transported their spies north, The Rundeen even began secretly including pureblood agents amongst its ranks. Privately, the Rundeen saw this as more of a mentorship program than an actual alliance, but would not admit this to the serpentfolk. Nevertheless, the Se'Sehen got their spies, thieves, and assassins operating across Faerûn, maintained their power against rival yuan-ti tribes in the Black Jungles, and advanced their plans to rule the surface world.

Similarly, the Sauringar, a weaker yuan-ti tribe who were close allies of the Se'Sehen, had worked with the Rundeen for several centuries, albeit in subtle and indirect ways. They supported the Rundeen's piracy and trade protection policies and in the 1300s DR discreetly discouraged them from choosing leaders who displayed the extreme ophiophobia that was customary among Tashalans. Thus it was that the Rundeen were ready to accept the alliance with the Se'Sehen, which in turn increased security for the Sauringar.

Enemies

In addition, the Rundeen had little in the way of enemies, for their only interests were mundane profits, markets, and trade. The majority of those who despised the organization were smaller-scale merchants forced to compete with better-supported Rundeen partners, and few were worthy of their notice, let alone their antagonism.

Similarly, the pashas of Calimshan had the power to counter the Rundeen in their realm, As of 1370 DR, the Yrshelem waited and watched to decide their course of action and evaded his spies by meeting always in a different place.

Naturally, there was also friction with powerful organized crime groups, who would either prey on their trade or compete with their own criminal enterprises. Among these were the Night Masks of Westgate, whose few real enemies included the Rundeen, Knights of the Shield, Men of the Basilisk, and other mercantile secret societies that had the resources and will to counter them at every opportunity, though none could harm them significantly either. The Cult of the Dragon and the Rundeen had also occasionally, albeit briefly, clashed over control of criminal trade. The Xanathar's Thieves' Guild's entry into Calimport was noticed but had not yet brought them into conflict. The Shadow Thieves of Amn named the Rundeen their greatest foe, being the biggest quasi-criminal power in Calimshan and thus the biggest impediment to their expansion south. But the Rundeen did not share their acrimony: they had different spheres of interest and influence when it came to crime and territory. The Yrshelem would be more interested in making a deal, such as surrendering some kinds of criminal activity for trade agreements or joining forces against Syl-Pasha Ralan, if given the chance. In fact, the Rundeen's offer of minor trading rights south of Amn for information and access to Maztica was rebuffed.

But perhaps their greatest enemies were the Harpers, while the Rundeen also figured highly among the many foes of Those Who Harp. The Harpers opposed the Tradesmen for their slaving and monopolizing, while the Rundeen despised them for regularly disrupting their plans, Thus, when they were discovered, the Rundeen were quick to seize any opportunity to attack and eliminate Harpers, capture and interrogate them before killing them, sabotage their plans, and destroy their bases. They kept their mercenary and adventuring bands on retainer for this reason.

History

Origins

The origins and past deeds of the Rundeen were little known and few scholars worked to uncover it—many of those who tried were found dead, particularly members of the Harpers and Heralds of Faerûn. Moreover, due to their later connection, some incorrectly theorized it was an offshoot of the Knights of the Shield, owing to their entwined history and certain similarities, namely both being secret societies and having similar greeting gestures. that emerged during the later period of the Shoon Imperium, sometime before the 6 century DR, at least as far as could be safely ascertained. In fact, they were even older, most likely having been around since the empire's peak.

Thus, the majority of studies began and ended at the etymology of their name, which was formerly written as Rulnadeen or Rulndiin in old records before simplifying to Rundeen over time: ruln was a Dwarvish word roughly translated as 'safety from fear of theft', and deen was a generic root word for 'coin' in use at the time of the Shoon Imperium. Hence it was the 'Safe Coin' consortium, focused on the protection of members and their assets and riches over long and hazardous trade expeditions. Extant shipping records of the time made mention of their heavily armed cargo ships escorted by swift military vessels, little better than corsairs and raiders, that launched preemptive attacks on rival shipping in order to secure their own. Although this increased costs, such ruthless and aggressive protection saw the Rulnadeen, or Rulndiin, dominate rival merchants. Their habits would change little over the next eight centuries, except when pressed to by outside forces.

In the , the Syl-Pasha of Calimshan, Akkabar Shoon the Elder, arranged for pirates to murder almost the entire Tethyrian royal family, paving the way for his coup and the establishment of the Shoon Imperium. He tried to frame the deaths on the Rundeen, then an up-and-coming merchant cabal, but without success.

One known early member of the Rundeen during the time of the Shoon Imperium was a notorious pirate and slaver named Malavar.

The Coin and the Shield

In time, the Shoon Imperium stretched across the Shining Sea to the northern coast of the Chultan Peninsula, arriving in Chult and the Confederation of Tashtan when they conquered the Tashtan cities in the . When the Knights of the Shield, then controlling a network of trade information-broking in Calimshan and Tethyr, tried to expand their influence to these new markets, they were rebuffed by the Rulnadeen. Upon investigating their new opponents, the Knights realized that it would be much simpler to agree to the merchants' demands and join them as members than to get rid of them by force. They allied and the Rulnadeen quickly began extending northward. Being more experienced in trading with foreign powers, several Knights quickly became high-ranking members of the Rulnadeen while the Tashalaran group used its new contacts to gain a foothold in Calimshan. At this time, the Mitalibbar switched the names of their ranks from Tashalan coins to Shoon Imperium coins.

Even when the Shoon Imperium was weak and divided under Qysari Shaani (–), the alliance of the Tradesmen and Knights meant that trade continued to flow from the Chultan Peninsula into the core of the Imperium and back.

In the final years of the Shoon Imperium, the leaders of the Rulnadeen began offering affiliate memberships in exchange for dues to various merchant houses, allowing the Rulnadeen to make bigger profits for less work.

When the Shoon Imperium finally fell in the , both the Rulnadeen and the Knights saw an opportunity to fill the power vacuum left behind. with the empire's collapse, they'd lost or given up their southern possessions, lost control of trade over the Shining Sea, and so no longer held sway amongst the Rulnadeen. In contrast, it was the Rulnadeen who were gaining the upper hand in Calimshan and kept their businesses there. The Rulnadeen and the Knights of the Shield were entirely separate organizations once more.

In the aftermath, without their former allies as rivals, without political stability in the area, and without imperial soldiers to enforce peace between merchants and prevent piracy, the Rulnadeen were perfectly positioned to fill the power vacuum and unilaterally create and enforce their own mercantile law. They discontinued simply attacking all rival shipping and instead only attacked some rival shipping and shared their protection with their affiliate members and any who'd give them a cut of the profits. As they were the only group to offer any protection, more and more merchants, preferring to lose some profit than all of it, opted for such dubious sanctuary with the Rulnadeen and their protection racket. And those who declined struggled to compete with the swelling Rulnadeen fleets and to avoid their attacks. Soon, only ships protected by the Rulnadeen were safe to travel in Calishite waters. Moreover, the Rulnadeen could delegate routine voyages to other companies and reserve riskier but more profitable expeditions for themselves. This practice would continue even into the 1300s DR.

Thus, in the , the Rulnadeen established a monopoly on all trade into and out of every port in Calimshan, around the Shining Sea, and along the Chultan Peninsula, where they also dominated the slave trade. With the fall of the Shoon Imperium, the Rundeen succeeded them as the primary architects of the slave trade, and even kept the cruel practice going long after it might've been broken and abandoned as unprofitable. In this time, their name also distorted into 'Rundeen', partly due to simplicity, partly due to outsiders' mispronunciation.

In a major stratagem to disrupt and seize control of Calishite trade, in the , the Rundeen arranged for ships sailing under false colors to arrive in the harbors of Calimport and Almraiven, take berths deep amongst other vessels and near shore, and then, by magic, explode. The resulting Port Fires devastated both cities, razing all docks, shipyards, and port warehouses and many other structures, not to mention claiming many lives.

The Fist of Rundeen, an elite band of assassins used to eliminate threats and traitors when deemed absolutely necessary, were formed some time in the 11 century DR.

The Rage of Wizards conflict, fought between two dozen ambitious archmages of Tashluta and Lapaliiya, who fought the yuan-ti of the Coiled Cabal, in the , momentarily shook the Rundeen's control of Tashalar, but did not break it.

In the early 1200s DR, Rundeen assassins murdered the vizar and wizard of Almraiven, Halasahr the Red, and his favorite jhasina (concubine) in their sleep. During the attack, the jhasina's enchanted slippers were also stolen.

The Treacherous Stab of the Harpers

Of course, this dominance drew the attention of meddlesome (that is, good) outsiders and many such organizations endeavored to liberate the port cities from their iron grasp. Among these groups were the Harpers, who would try to thwart their monopolizing, piracy, and slavery. However, the Rundeen were always able to fend them off or redirect them at their rivals and the rest of the time were content to ignore them. Nevertheless, some Tradesmen held a grudge and established the various anti-Harper mercenary companies. They would go on to have several clashes, but were defeated several times by the Harpers, who saw them as no longer effective by 1367 DR.

But then, in the , came an attack they could not shrug off.

The Rundeen leaders dubbed this attack "the Treacherous Stab of the Harpers" and sought revenge.

Around 1352 DR, the current nirdey of Manshaka, a caravaneer named Rahimat yi Manshaka, revealed 'himself' to actually be a woman named Rafi el Shuree. Impressing leadership with her skill and audacity, she was appointed Grand Yrshelem of Caravans only two years later in the .

In the , the Clenched Fist fought a band of Harpers including the bard Mintiper Moonsilver, who had nothing good to say about them.

Modern History

In the , the then Grand Yrshelem of Ships died of old age, so the others appointed Zuras el Ireen, a successful Rundeen shipping businessman and former pirate to the position.

The mass death of Bhaal-worshiping assassins during the Time of Troubles in the , gutted the Shadow Thieves who'd once dominated Calimshan's underworld. In only a few months, the Rundeen and eight other criminal organizations (including the Dark Dagger and some beholden to the Twisted Rune) moved in and carved it up between them, maintaining a new, uneasy balance of power for almost two years. But the following year, the Calishite authorities caught up and tried to muscle in, triggering the Darkstalker Wars for control of the criminal underworld. This served as cover for the rise to power of Ralan el Pesarkhal. Over the course of the 1360s DR, Syl-Pasha Ralan el Pesarkhal united the cities of Calimshan under his rule. With this, the Rundeen lost their grip on Calimport and Almraiven and their monopoly on shipping across the Shining Sea. Nevertheless, their continuing control of other cities let them retain their monopolies in several industries.

In the , the First Yrshelem of Slaving retired and passed the position onto Saibhon el Bhiras, an enterprising slaver and businessman.

Material for Volo's Guide to Calimport was written over 1364 to 1365 DR by the Master Traveler, Volothamp Geddarm, but all final drafts were destroyed by both Rundeen agents and Calishite pashas, so it went officially unreleased.

Fortunately, at least for them, the Rundeen uncovered a band of young Harpers passing through Myratma in the , and had them kidnapped, and presumed murdered. The group had been journeying northward to arrange aid for the Janessar in their rebellion and slave emancipation, and carried information on them. That year, the Grand Yrshelem of Slaves retired; Saibhon el Bhiras was promoted to the position.

As of the , to make up for their losses, the Rundeen had begun to be more proactive and visible in their deeds The Rundeen and the Knights of the Shield would reestablished their alliance by 1373 DR. In addition, the Rundeen patiently but closely monitored Pesarkhal, understanding they were a barrier to his goal of amassing all political, mercantile, and criminal power in Calimshan. Their options ranged from an alliance, but deemed this unlikely given his ruthless habit of disposing of those no longer of value to him, to an assassination. The Yrshelem gambled that their anonymity would protect them and that threatening Pesarkhal might persuade him they were too big to try to bring down.

Meanwhile, the growing trade from Maztica attracted the attention of the Grand Yrshelem of Ships, Zuras el Ireen, but he found it still too hazardous for both Rundeen trade vessels and pirates and elected to wait until the route was safer. Nevertheless, the Rundeen contacted a few known members of the Shadow Thieves of Amn, seeking information and access to Maztica in exchange for some minor trading rights to Amn's south, but this deal was rejected.

Similarly, the Rundeen's interest in Amn was threatened by the emergence of the Sythillisian Empire, a monstrous humanoid kingdom seizing land in southern Amn. While the Rundeen avoided dealing with armies and non-humans, they did mull over making a deal with the empire's ruling ogre magi, Sythillis and Cyrvisnea: Rundeen pirates like the Sea Reavers would blockade Amnian ports like Murann to prevent treasure ships fleeing, in exchange for a cut of that treasure, as well as stop the Amnians retaking the city.

At some point, Rundeen slavers on a raid in Tashalar ran into yuan-ti from the Black Jungles, and attacked. However, these yuan-ti, hailing from the Se'Sehen tribe, were on something of a fact-finding mission about human organizations and trade and held the slavers off until they heard them out. Thereafter, they had good relations and pureblood ambassadors accompanied the Rundeen slavers to meet with the mitalibbar of Calimport, and thence the Yrshelem. The yuan-ti proposed a limited alliance: for a supply of slaves and the chance to work alongside and study the Rundeen's human members, they would offer exotic trade goods and maybe even arcane lore. If the Se'Sehen could use what they learned to produce their own goods and invigorate their culture, this arrangement had the option of becoming a proper trade alliance. Intrigued by the possibilities, the Yrshelem agreed and, over the course of 1370 DR, a good number of yuan-ti appeared amongst the Rundeen membership,

At some point, Rundeen-affiliated pirates caused the deaths of two sons of the syl-pasha of Calimshan, Ralan el Pesarkhal, as well as the young wife of a third son, Tahyr.

There came another outrage in 1370 DR when the husband of Fatima el Galsah—the eldest daughter of Ralan el Pesarkhal—vanished from Almraiven and Vizar Majel el Arpooristan accused Rundeen trader and suspected slaver Kyahj el Haedi of arranging the man's kidnap and sale into Underdark slavery in the Alimir Hive. While Kyahj firmly protested his innocence, Fatima also blamed the leaders of the Rundeen-controlled Vyndahla clan. This led to fears of an imminent war between factions in Almraiven. Meanwhile, Fatima sought adventurers to rescue her husband from the beholders.

In the spring of the , there was a surprise wedding between Farid al Nisr, the son of First Yrshelem Fahd al Nisr (and a Harper), and Syllia Adarbrent, daughter of House Adarbrent of Waterdeep (and also a Harper). Their families did not have long to be shocked before the pair sailed off to Maztica with no clue when they might return. House Adabrent were masters of the Adarbrent Shipping Company and the Horizon Armada league of shipowners, yet were struggling financially. Seizing an opportunity to expand and claim these northern shipping fortunes, Fahd contacted Lord Royus Adarbrent with offers of generous loans at very reasonable rates, if they but signed a contract with the Rundeen. Unfamiliar with the southern merchants, Royus considered this reluctantly and cautiously. Via a go-between, he would hire unconnected adventurers to look al Nisr business dealings first.

Meanwhile, by Shieldmeet of that year, a series of assassinations and blackmails of the princes of Tashalar's ruling families had rocked the realm. Many suspected the Rundeen or yuan-ti or lesser merchant families to be behind this, with the ultimate goal being to install a noble as overall ruler of the realm who would serve as their puppet. Whatever the truth, all factions involved began hiring mercenaries and adventurers for either their own defense or to make counter-attacks on suspected foes.

But whatever their plans, following the Spellplague of the , a vast stretch of Tashalar was submerged beneath the sea, while the genies Calim and Memnon and their genasi followers took over Calimshan, reshaping the realm. And so, after centuries of operation, the Rundeen faded from history.
