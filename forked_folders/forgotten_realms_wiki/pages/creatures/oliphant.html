Oliphants were believed to be descend from the ancient Pleistocene mastodons, sharing many of their primal traits.

Description

Its thick, shaggy coat allowed it to thrive in cold temperate zones and subarctic regions. Preferring isolation, the oliphant typically kept its distance from human settlements. However, if captured during its youth, it could be domesticated and trained.

Behavior

Oliphants did not fear fire unless it directly threatened them. They were aggressive and tended to eliminate threats rather than flee.

Combat

Oliphants possessed considerable intelligence that, when harnessed through proper training, transformed them into formidable instruments of war—particularly the massive males. With attentive care and nourishment, these beasts could reach towering sizes of ten feet or more, far surpassing their untamed kin in both stature and strength. In battle, they were outfitted with elaborate armor—feathers, scales, and plated defenses—along with vicious spikes jutting from their heads and forelimbs. Upon their backs, fortress-like houdas housed squads of archers and pikemen, turning each oliphant into a mobile stronghold. While elephants in warmer regions were also equipped for combat, they lacked the savage intensity and disciplined fury that defined the trained oliphant.

An oliphant engaged foes using its downward-curving tusks and the sheer force of its powerful forelegs. Its trunk, though present, lacked sufficient length to serve as a practical weapon in combat.

Paladins could use this creature as their mount.

Ecology

Oliphants could be encountered solitary or as a 'family' with calves (typically up to 8).

Usage

Oliphant tusks were valued between 100 and 400 gold pieces apiece, while their ivory typically fetched around 4 gp per pound on the open market.
