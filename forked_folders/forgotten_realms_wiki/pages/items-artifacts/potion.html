Potions, also known as elixirs or drafts, were types of liquid crafted by alchemists or spellcasters with intended magical or extraordinary effects, with some copying the effects of spells. Some would even say they were "spells in liquid form", with their imbibers being both the effective target and the caster, but without requiring any of the necessary skill in the Art.

Description

Potions were typically stored within vessels made of either glass, ceramic, alabaster, crystal, or metal. flasks,

The actual "liquid" component of potions varied widely in appearance, taste, and odor. Being that they were liquids, it was extremely difficult to use potions whilst underwater. Some got around this by creating pockets of air or storing them within a potion bladder, specially fitter with a flexible stem and capped nozzle. When needing to imbibe, one would pinch the stem and keep it as such until they had removed the cap and put the nozzle to their lips.

Potions always radiated a detectable aura of magic, which could be analyzed to determine the school of magic of its effect. items like a rod of cancellation, and dead-magic zones. However, the effects of a potion would continue to function if imbibed prior to entering a dead-magic zone.

Creation

More so than in any other aspect of sorcery, there were many ways to achieve a desired potion. The ingredients needed to brew a potion varied with each effect, but it was always necessary to have more than one ingredient. These could include such things as gemstones, magical oils, water drawn from sacred sites, hydrae organs, roc eggs, and enchanted herbs, as well as mundane herbs, spices, and vegetables. The medicinal benefits of such plants often served as an indicator of what kind of magical effects they could be used to achieve. Plants could be incorporated into a potion as either extracts, distillates, in a dried form, or in a crushed powder form.

In terms of gemstones, frost agate and moss agate were commonly used in the brewing of all manner of potions, for they had an effect of stabilizing substances that would otherwise be volatile if mixed together.

Producers

Texts pre-dating the Year of Wild Magic often considered the brewing of potions to be the providence of alchemists, wizards, and priests. Some would even claim that specialty priests and wizards were limited by whatever their sphere or school of magic was. Though the craft was also not simply limited to spellcasters, as many alchemists, and herbalists were found to brew potions.

In general, priests were known for brewing potions with effects relating to healing and cures. The Lifewardens of Chauntea could brew practically potion involving healing and plants. And mystic wanderers were known to be quite skilled in the arts of potion brewing, being that they devoted themselves to life-long studies of the medicinal and poisonous uses of various fungi and plants.

Alchemists who were also arcane or divine spellcasters could go on to become master alchemists, individuals specialized in the art of brewing potions, becoming so skilled that they could manage to brew two to four potions in a single day. Some alchemists joined the Church of Mystra, becoming apothecars, specialty priests skilled at both brewing and identifying potions. And self-taught mages, known as hedge-wizards, in many cases were found to learn the art of brewing potions sooner than proper mages.

Wizards with dedicated laboratories often used all manner of tools in their brewing, such as alembics, beakers, braziers, furnaces, and retorts. And priests often did their brewing upon consecrated altars, using the tools of faith and prayer.

In the time between the Spellplague and the Second Sundering, many spellcasters performed the concocting of potions through means of a ritual called brew potion.

Availability

  • In the Realms, potions made up a reliable source of income for a great many hermits, traveling preachers, priests who tended shrines, and temples. This was due in part to such makers keeping some of their potion formulae a secret, thereby ensuring that people would have to rely upon them for more.
  • In Dark Hollow, a village in the Vast, many hedge-wizards made their day-to-day living by selling potions.
  • Eldathyn clerics often served local communities as potion-makers.
  • In the Underdark, traveling merchants were a common source of magical potions. Potions were also major products of the cities Ched Nasad, Menzoberranzan, and Sshamath.
  • Many wizards sold their potions at Magefairs, whilst others, known as trade wizards, made a living by producing potions with minor effects in bulk to sell at reasonable prices.

Alternatives

Beneath the waves of Faerûn's Inner Sea, the aquatic races of Serôs developed their potions in the form of viscous substances, like a thick gelatinous paste of kelp, so as to get around the hurdles of imbibing in a fluid volume. They would typically carry these "potions" around in either clam shells or flexible tubes made from eelskins.

In other parts of the Realms, aquatic elves likewise devised their own alternatives to potions. Whilst some were akin to pastes or unguents, others cultivated magical sea fruits and seaweed.

On the desert world of Athas, water was more precious than gold and thus too valuable to be wasted on potions. In their place, the Athasians developed potion fruit, fruits and berries that contained potion-like effects in their juices. Some individuals on Toril, like Fent-Moyer Thrist Hauser of the Vast, developed trees that similarly bore fruits with potion-like effects.
