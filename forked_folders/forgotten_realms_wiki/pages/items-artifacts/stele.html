Stele

A stele ( ) were slabs Often made of stone, sometimes of an ornamental nature, to funerary purposes, or to document or commemorating historical events or personages.

Notable Menhirs

  • In Avernus, the Crypt of the Hellriders held four memorial steles, inscribed necromantically with the names of all the Hellriders who fell whilst fighting at Zariel's side. These steles binded the spirits of those Hellriders, tethering them to the crypt and thus forcing them to linger on as ghosts. However, the steles were protected by necrotic energy so long as the soul-bound parchments in the crypt were unharmed.
  • In the ancient city-state of Cursrah, stelae were used for funerary purposes.
  • In the Chultan Peninsula, the ancient yuan-ti city of Ssy'in'tia'saminass had stone inscribed with Draconic runes.
  • Gravenhollow, a mystical library in the Underdark, kept some of its ancient records inscribed upon the surfaces of stelae.
  • In north Faerûn, the over a dozen ogre tribes of Thar used stone stelae to mark their territory. They were pieces of notable artistry, carved with all manner of subject matter, from the ogre god Vaprak vanquishing his enemies, to ancient heroes and monsters, The art style of carvings was noted by some as being reminescent of carvings found further west, among the Ffolk and the Northmen of the Moonshae Isles. These stelae were also frequently inscribed with an ornamental runic script, potentially the remnants of ancient written language among the ogres of Thar, and each tribe had its own signature rune. Whilst art collectors would likely pay a great deal for them, the ogres were highly protective of their stelae, even going so far as to pursue thieves beyond Thar. Additionally, many of them were imbued with curses by tribal shamans.
  • In the Plane of Fire, the City of Brass had a road famous for the stelae that lined it, known as the Street of Stelae. The stelae that lined it were inscribed with the victories of the efreeti over the other races of the plane of fire, as well as the spoils they claimed.
  • Deep in the jungles of Far Payit, around 80 stelae known as "tree-stones" were erected all throughout the Ixtzul Valley. Outside of the city of Ixtzul, these columns of white granite stood and were carved with ornate, ornamental glyphs. At the tops of the colemns resided a stylized head of H'Calos the Star Worm, a strange creature from beyond the stars who was trapped beneath the city of Ixtzul with a great binding spell. These stelae were tied to the preservation of this binding spell and as such radiated a strong magical aura.
  • In the Haunted Lands of Zakhara, the people of Sokkar used steles for funerary purposes. Regardless of the deceased's station in life, tombs in Sokkar always featured both steles and a ka figure. They were typically decorated in intricate carvings depicting scenes of the deceased dining, hunting, praying, sleeping, praying, or playing what were their favorite games and sporting activities in life. Whilst the ka figures were believed to hold a deceased's ka (soul), lining the walls of a tomb with these stone slabs were said to ensure that the deceased could perform the depicted enjoyable activities throughout their time in the afterlife.
  • In the Outlands, the gate-town of Rigus had large granite steles erected as memorials, engraved with the names of fallen heroes.

History

When Edimu, a beloved horse from Jazirat al-Sayyad, passed from the world a mausoleum was constructed for him in Sokkar. Placed within this mausoleum was a stele, depicting Edimu galavanting in the hills of a fertile island, along with an inscription in Chun that labeled it "Jazirat al-Sayyad." It was accompanied by a life-sized ka figure made of alabaster, adorned with a golden bit and bridle. The bridle was encrusted in diamonds and sapphires, as well as inscribed with Chun runes that bore Edimu's name and wishes for a long, happy afterlife. However, ghouls later looted the chambers and stole the horse's bit and bridle, breaking both his ka figure and stele in the process, and thus untethering his spirit from the afterlife.
