The Ffolk were the first humans of the Moonshae Isles, which they had come to share with their longtime enemies, the Northlanders. The Ffolk traditionally inhabited the southern parts of the Moonshaes, including Flamsterd, Moray, Snowdown, and Sunset, and the southern parts of Alaron and Gwynneth.

Description

The Ffolk were dark-haired and fair-skinned, although considered to have darker skin than that of the Northlanders. They favored tunics of earthy colors, sometimes decorated with embroidered patterns of repeating lines or symbols.

The first Ffolk were Talfiric refugees who settled the Moonshaes before adopting the name of "the Ffolk".

Outlook

The Ffolk were fiercely independent, valuing self-reliance, individual responsibility, and finding one's own way in the world. Surviving in the harsh lands of the Moonshaes was difficult to do without help, and so the Ffolk had come to be staunchly loyal to their families, friends, and neighbors. Most Ffolk-run businesses could be expected to provide good quality for a reasonable price.

They had great reverence for nature, and tried to avoid using or taking more from their land than they needed. Many Ffolk also held a deep reverence for the sea and for the art of sailing.

Culture

The Ffolk held to a strong druidic tradition that was distinct from that of the druids in mainland Faerûn. Their society was mainly agricultural and was ruled by a High King or Queen who resided in Caer Callidyrr, the capital of the Ffolk. They were isolationist and rejected any efforts by mainlanders to influence their culture. Rural Ffolk were known to reject what they saw as material excesses.

The Ffolk spent most of their time on peaceful pastimes such as farming, fishing, hunting, and trading. Ffolk crafters were known for their pottery, woolen textiles, steel weapons, and shipbuilding. Their shipwrights specialized in a type of ship that was slow but designed to withstand some of the roughest seas. Ffolk artists were renowned as far as Thay for designs resembling intricate knots.

Martial Traditions

Despite their mostly peaceful lifestyle, valor and strength in battle were highly respected traits among the Ffolk, even if they usually wished to see these skills put to use in pursuit of peace. Ffolk warriors favored leather armor and were well-matched in skill to their Northmen rivals, but even a mob of Ffolk commoners could be brutal and bloodthirsty when faced with a hated enemy. It was not an uncommon practice to claim the heads or skulls of fallen enemies as trophies. A "warrior's death" in battle was regarded as the most honorable way to die. Some Ffolk honored and derived authority from their noble or martial ancestors, and their most respected champions could even summon long-dead ancestral defenders.

Disputes of honor were not often settled with violence, but with contests of athletic ability or stamina. These might include logging competitions or races. Ffolk respected warriors who embodied quiet strength rather than overt violence.

Religion

The Ffolk worshiped the Earthmother, and venerated her and the land itself as one and the same. They had a deep reverence for nature and the land around them, always respecting the divine power in a misty forest, a rushing river, or a hidden pool.

The Earthmother was often regarded as an aspect of Chauntea, however many Ffolk did not ascribe to this belief. Their dogged monotheism could cause tension when they traveled among the polytheistic followers of the Faerûnian pantheon.

Language

The language of the Ffolk was Waelan. In this language, "the Ffolk" literally translated to "the People".

By the late 14 century DR, many Ffolk spoke Illuskan and employed the Thorass script. and by the late 15 century DR, many also spoke Chondathan and the Elven language.

Common Ffolk male names included Artur, Bern, Colin, Manfred, and Tristan. Common female names included Alicia, Gennifer, Meridith, Elaine, and Olivia. Common surnames included Archer, Gareth, Leed, Morgan, and Waters. Most noble or royal families of the Ffolk had names that were evolutions or perversions of the ancient dynastic name of Cymrych, including Cambridge, Carrathal, Cyric, Kendrick, Kimball, Kincaid, Riker, and Selkirk.

Magic

Arcane magic, derisively called "sorcery", was regarded with extreme superstition, suspicion, and apprehension by the Ffolk, and the only places in their lands that wizards could practice in assured safety were on Flamsterd and under the auspices of the council of mages who served the High King. Because Ffolk communities traditionally lacked any priests or clerics, even healing magic and other druidic or divine magics might be confused for hated sorcery. On the other hand, the magic inherent in a bard's music did not arouse such concerns.

Among those Ffolk who did pursue studies in wizardry, their approach to magic was heavily influenced by their upbringing. Ffolk reverence for nature meant that these mages often specialized in herbalism or in magics relevant to agriculture.

Entertainment

Ffolk festivals were hard-drinking affairs featuring music and acrobats. Their parties, including funerals, entailed huge feasts that transformed into nights of dancing and music until the guests fell asleep at the venue.

Traditional Ffolk instruments included cymbals, pipes, and the rare harp. Their most notable songs included the Ballad of Cymrych Hugh, the Song of the Earthmother, the Song of the Llewyrr, and the Song of the North Wind. Bards of the Ffolk could attain celebrity status.

Relationships

Most Ffolk lived in small communities that were relatively isolated from the outside world, meaning that they were accustomed to relying only on themselves or their close neighbors. When mercenaries from Amn subsequently seized the island of Snowdown in the 15 century DR, the Ffolk's hatred turned toward the Amnians; the Ffolk were generally skeptical of mainlanders, but were most welcoming to Waterdhavians and Baldurians owing to their longstanding trade with them. They held to a number of superstitious beliefs and traditions relating to fey and to followers of Malar.

History

The first Ffolk to appear on the Moonshae Isles were members of the lost tribes of Talfir who fled Ebenfar to escape the Shadowking Verraketh. They were said to have first arrived on the island of Alaron before founding their first settlement on the island of Gwynneth in the , naming that island for the woman who had led them there. They soon expanded to the other Moonshae Isles, first Alaron, Moray, and Snowdown and then north to Norland and the Norheim Isles. They soon came into conflict with the Llewyrr elves, who would gradually retreat to more and more remote parts of the isles.

After defeating the creature known as Kazgoroth, Cymrych Hugh was crowned as the first High King of the Ffolk in the . Although nominally united, the Ffolk were in fact divided amongst many squabbling subkingdoms. Even so, they were the undisputed rulers of the Moonshaes up until the arrival of Illuskan raiders (who would come to be known as the Northlanders) challenged their hold on the northern isles beginning in the . This led to centuries of conflict and wars with the two groups.

In the , Ffolk colonized the Utter East and enslaved the native Mar of the area, carving it up into the Five Kingdoms.

The reign of the Cymrych Dynasty of High Kings came to an end in the , with the death of High King Tanner, at which point the Ffolk were finally forced to cede the northern Moonshaes to the Northlanders.

The Ffolk would not be reunited under a true High King until the rise of Tristan Kendrick in the mid–14 century DR, who recovered the ancient symbols of the High King and then brokered a peace with the Northlanders known as the Treaty of Oman in the . At the same time, High Queen Alicia worked to erode the Ffolk's longstanding preference for isolation from the mainland.

By the late 15 century DR, with threats and invaders causing strife across the Moonshaes, it was not uncommon for the fiercely self-reliant Ffolk to take up arms as adventurers. When the rural Ffolk of Alaron were forced from their homes by Amnian mercenaries and evil fey from the Dernall Forest in the , many became independent adventurers and rallied as a Ffolk rearguard, seeking to fight for their homes rather than to flee.
