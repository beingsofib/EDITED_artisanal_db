Tuilvilanuue

Language
nameTuilvilanuue
familyElven
speakersRaptorans

Tuilvilanuue was a language spoken by raptorans that was derived from Elven.

Description

Tuilvilanuue was fluid and lyrical in sound, and though it contained many lengthy and tongue-twisting words, its grammar was simple, or at least simpler than Elven. It instead depended on inflection to convey a good deal of meaning. Thus, a spoken conversation in Tuilvilanuue was very clear and efficient, but with subtle aspects of intonation that were difficult for non-raptorans to fully understand.

Script

As Tuilvilanuue was derived from Elven it used the Espruar alphabet for written communication. However, due to the strong emphasis on inflection, the meaning of a text tended to lack the clarity of spoken Tuilvilanuue. This led raptoran authors to write several sentences to express the nuances of a passage. As a result, written works by raptorans were often rather long, with things often explained three or four times.

In any case, few Tuilvilanuue writings were made for the simple reason that raptorans found books to be heavy and difficult to carry when flying. Being practically minded, they reserved their writing for simple messages and preferred to memorize their poems, songs, and oral histories. This led to the creation of the raptoran saying, "Memories are lighter to carry than books."

Phrases

Lies va fliesta e flies va liesta.: "Run from things that fly and fly from things that run." This phrase simply meant that it's best to run from a predator with a method of travel that said predator doesn't have.

Na twa dima menti sintis, mil onit hista.: "When two minds think alike, only one is necessary." This phrase expressed the raptorans' love of debate and their value of the individual and ability to think freely.

Fristi milta orisa nifint.: "Anger is the one thing that grows in confinement." This warned of the danger of bottling up one's anger and also reminded the listener that freedom was necessary for growth.

Nilsi hentil kilta anno.: "It takes four seasons to make a year." This saying was a reminder that there's a proper time for everything and that one should prepare for what is to come next.

Menti lintu onlita venla op avel donlita venila.: "A mind opened by wonder is clearer than one closed by belief." Another saying that showcased raptorans' love of debate. This saying asked the listener to seek a fresh outlook and not to believe too strongly in just one belief.

Viilit nesla odelmin e van rensii, sil ti gar, odelmin vimil ginrinit.: "The mouse fears the rising of the moon. But for the owl, moonrise means it's time to hunt." This one also had a double meaning. Most obviously, it's better to be the hunter than the hunted. The deeper meaning to the saying, however, was that the timid fear change while the courageous embrace it.

Seniil limna!: "From the sky!" This was the most common battle phrase of raptorans, who delivered it even when not flying.

Wylintasec mendilsi mentisinecta, sil viltima rentis da silliit altina.: "Stupidity is a wound that bleeds forever." This was another variation of the raptorans' disdain for inflexible thinking.

Vastikkimensa hileka belinnit e hastal min resinatadol, sil fostil nasila fenes e mastinnit.: "The careful hunter chooses well and finishes with a full belly, but the fool wastes arrows and goes hungry." This is the raptoran's version of the common saying "haste makes waste" and showcases their desire to carefully think through things before coming to a decision.

Niffimat orisa vilrad, sil nolim tramankit.: "A bad seed puts forth a green shoot, but seldom a strong tree." Like many other raptoran sayings, this one has a double meaning. On one hand, it warned that while a plan that worked well initially could still go wrong or that the first result might be a fluke. It also warned against receiving gifts from bad actors as it would be unlikely to work out well in the long run.

Benisiim iltem rantoli rah silet nasilit. Wisilti maneti silti rah silet veniin.: "The generous give more than they can. The proud take less than they need." This phrase simply stated what the raptorans' view of a good life is: to support one's flock as much as possible but also try to demand as little as possible from others.

Notable Speakers

The red great wyrm Imvaernarhro spoke Tuilvilanuue.
